Assignment Sheet

For your next assignment, you will design the assignment sheet for our course final project. When you’re finished, we will hold a vote to decide whose assignment sheet we should use for the final project.

Prewriting

Before preparing an assignment sheet, you must ask yourself: What do I want my students to get out of this assignment? What writing skills do they need to develop further? What sort of writing activity will help them develop those skills?

Ideally, you will already have a sense of what you and your classmates need to work on; if you’re stuck, you might take a look at the learning objectives on our syllabus. Pick one or two, and think of an assignment that will help build those skills.

Evaluation Style

Once you’ve come up with the basic concept for your assignment, you will need to decide how it should be evaluated. If your assignment is selected by the class, I will follow your evaluative criteria when I grade.

Personally, I like assignment sheets with only a small number of relatively open-ended criteria, which I call “Requirements.” Some writing instructors prefer to follow an extremely rigid rubric. Some grade based on the amount of revision, rather than the quality of the final product. How do you think writing should be evaluated, and how will you design fair but achievable criteria by which your assignment should be assessed?

Writing the Assignment Sheet

After you’ve planned out the above, you’re ready to begin writing. As always, you should consider your audience when putting this assignment sheet together: what will your classmates need to know in order to have a clear-cut conception of what they should do to successfully complete the assignment? What background information do they need? Will they be familiar with the genre you’re asking them to write in? Is there anything they could read/look at as a model example? Will they be able to draw on past experiences, or will your assignment be completely unfamiliar terrain?

Requirements

  • Invent an interesting, achievable writing task, appropriate for this classroom
  • Set clear-cut expectations for how the assignment should be evaluated
  • Use the assignment to develop a writing skill you think is important
  • Include a one-sentence description of the assignment (a “tl;dr”) at the end of your assignment sheet. We will use this sentence to cast our votes.

Fine Print

This assignment is worth 10% of your final grade. The first draft is due April 18 on Eli Review. There will then be a peer review session due on April 20. The final draft should be submitted on paper on April 26. The final draft should be 750 words.
