Defining Rhetoric

For the previous assignment, you deployed the terms genre, purpose, and audience in order to analyze a digital composition you created. These three terms are central to the study of rhetoric, which, along with writing, is the subject of this course.

For your next assignment, you will write a definition of one key rhetorical term. After you have submitted your final drafts, I will compile them into a reference document, which will then be available to you as a resource as we continue to use these terms for the rest of the semester.

You and the members of your peer group will be defining the same term; in consequence, peer review will give you a chance not only to offer feedback, but also to examine the task of defining a word from a variety of perspectives.

Why Are We Doing This?

As you know, we live in a search engine age; “Google it” is sage wisdom for the uninformed. But someone, somewhere took the time to write all that helpful information out – to think through and compile the dictionary and encyclopedia entries, the how-to guides, and the Shmoop articles we feel we couldn’t live without.

Their service enables us to live, and their intellectual labor saves us a great deal of thinking. So let’s see what it’s like to do what they do for us.

The Research Process

You will, of course, need to read a little bit about the term you’ve been assigned before you begin writing your definition. I’ll give you some things to read, but I think it would be best if you sought out reference materials of your own. Google (or better, DuckDuckGo) is your friend, but I would encourage you to make use of the UB Library Catalogue as you search. I also find Library Genesis a useful resource when I am trying to get a book in a hurry.

Take notes as you read around, and remember to record where your information comes from. Since you will be defining your term in your own words, it would be a good idea to read many definitions and explanations of it, so that you develop a broad general sense of what it means. But if you come across a particularly useful way of phrasing something, copy it down as a quotation in your notes.

Plagiarism

This assignment lends itself to “plagiarism,” which means presenting someone else’s words or ideas as your own. If you feel you do not fully understand how to avoid plagiarism (a common difficulty), please reach out to me.

Some Thoughts on Definitions: Genre and Appearance

Reference materials like dictionaries and encyclopedias present information in a highly structured way. The most important information, along with any key terms, is placed at the beginning.

In terms of tone and word choice, your definition should be relatively formal, but accessible and understandable to an ordinary reader.

Definitions – especially of writing concepts – frequently feature a few examples that illustrate the concept in action. These are often positioned toward the end of the definition; they may even be sectioned off from the rest of the text by a header.

No concept is set in stone. Longer definitions frequently describe any debates or disagreements about the meaning of a term; your definition should do the same.

Requirements

Your paper should:

  • Imitate the tone, diction, and style of a typical definition, such as those found in dictionaries, encyclopedias, and other reference materials;
  • Be highly organized – in particular, featuring the key points first;
  • Illustrate the concept you are defining in a variety of ways – including explicit definitions; accounts of any debates about the meaning of the term; analogies to other, more familiar concepts; and a few examples of the concept in action;
  • Make use of typography to create user-friendliness – this might include using section headings, or bold and italic text;
  • Include a “Further Reading” section at the end, with one or two recommendations for someone to learn more about the concept;
  • Include a “Works Cited section, also at the end, which cites the sources you used when preparing your definition.
